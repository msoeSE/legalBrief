Brief Information

Issues presented is required.
The Appellant’s Initial Brief lists the Issues Presented in this appeal. These are the circuit court mistakes that the Appellant wants the Court of Appeals to review. If you, the Respondent, think that the Appellant has mischaracterized the Issues Presented, you may state them properly here. See Wis. Stat. §809.19(1)(b) regarding the Issues Presented.
Oral argument statement is required.
The Statement on Oral Argument section explains whether the Court of Appeals should hold a hearing where you and your opponent are allowed to speak. Usually, the Court of Appeals does not hold oral argument. Instead, it decides an appeal after reading the parties' briefs. The factors that the Court of Appeals considers in deciding whether to hold oral argument appear in Wis. Stat. §809.22 .
Publication statement is required.
The Statement on Publication section states whether you think the Court of Appeals should publish the decision in your case by printing it in a book that contains important decisions. The factors that the Court of Appeals considers in deciding whether to publish its decision appear in Wis. Stat. §809.23 .
Case and facts statement is required.
A Statement of Case and Facts describes the nature of the case and provides a short overview of what happened at the circuit court level—i.e. only those facts needed to understand and decide the arguments in the appeal. A Statement of Case and Facts is usually written in chronological order. It may only refer to facts that were previously presented to the circuit court. The Respondent is not required to include a Statement of Case and Facts in the Response Brief. However, if the Respondent disagrees with the Appellant’s Statement of Case and Facts, then the Respondent should present her version of events in her Response Brief. Each statement of fact must be followed by a citation to the Record on Appeal. For example, “R.35:26” is a citation to Item No. 35 in the Record on Appeal. The Statement of Case and Facts section is required by Wis. Stat.§809.19(1)(d) .
Argument is required.
The Argument section of a Response Brief should respond to each argument that Appellant made in his Initial Brief. The Respondent’s Argument should explain that the circuit court decided the case correctly. If the Respondent fails to respond to an argument in the Appellant’s Initial Brief, the Court of Appeals may hold that the Respondent conceded the point. The Respondent’s Argument should include one or more one-sentence “argument headings” followed by several paragraphs explaining why he should win that point. The Argument should be brief and clear. It should refer to case law, statutes, constitutional provisions and/or other regulations that support your point. The Brief Assistant will direct you to websites where you can find these legal resources. The Argument section is required by Wis. Stat.§809.19(1)(e) .
Conclusion is required.
This section should be very short. It should simply tell the Court of Appeals what you want it to do. For example, as the Respondent you probably want the Court of Appeals to affirm the circuit court’s decision. The Conclusion section is required by Wis. Stat.§809.19(1)(f) .
